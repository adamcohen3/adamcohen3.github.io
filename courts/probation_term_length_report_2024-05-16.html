Re-estimating Probation Term Lengths in Hawai’i

Author

Adam S. Cohen

Published

May 16, 2024

Abstract

How long are probation terms in Hawai’i? According to a report by The Pew Charitable Trusts (2020), the average probation term length is 58.9 months, the longest of any state in the US. But this analysis was limited to a single year, relied on an “indirect” approximation method, and suffered from at least one serious data quality issue that likely impacted estimates. To address these limitations, probation term length was estimated over a 12 year window with higher quality data, providing a more complete and accurate profile. Results show that probation term lengths range from 29.7 to 38.4 months. The new estimates converge when using the “indirect” method and a direct approach that relies on case-level data. Even when these limitations are addressed, estimates of probation term length are still misleading as they lump felony and non-felony probationers, which are largely non-overlapping subpopulations. To provide a more conceptually meaningful estimate of probation term length, estimates were disaggregated by offense degree, yielding 15.4 to 34.2 months for misdemeanants and 52.9 to 69.4 months for felons between 2012 and 2023. Limitations of the analysis and implications for shortening probation are discussed.

1 Background

In a state-by-state analysis of probation length across the US, The Pew Charitable Trusts reported that, in 2018, the state of Hawaii had the longest average probation lengths in the country at 58.9 months (The Pew Charitable Trusts, 2020).


Notably, the analysis focused on a single year, and not on the larger pattern across time that could reveal whether 2018 was representative of probation length in general or whether there has been change over time.

2 Probation length before and after 2018

To provide a more complete picture of probation length in Hawaii, we analyzed data prior to 2018 and since 2018 using the same data source and method as Pew. Pew relied on data from the Bureau of Justice Statistics Annual Probation Survey and an indirect method to estimate probation length (Patterson & Preston, 2008).

Expand to learn more about the indirect method used by Pew

Pew lacked access to case-level data (e.g., probation start and termination dates) that would be needed to calculate probation term length for individual probationers. Instead, they relied on descriptive statistics for the entire group of probationers and used those to approximate probation term length. This method has been shown to produce valid estimates so long as certain assumptions are met (Patterson & Preston, 2008).

Estimating probation length using this indirect method as described in Patterson and Preston (2008)) depends on only three variables: the size of the probation population at the beginning and end of the year, along with the number of exits. These three variables are used to calculate an “exit rate.”

\[exit.rate = \frac{exits}{\frac{1}{2}*(population_{begin} + population_{end})}\] The inverse of this exit rate is the estimated average probation length.

\[probation.length = \frac{1}{exit.rate}\]

To pump your intuition for why this works, consider a case where the probation population at the beginning of the year is 10,000 and by the end of the year it remains at 10,000.

First, imagine a situation where there are 10,000 exits during the year. Then, \(exit.rate = \frac{10,000}{\frac{1}{2}*(10,000+10,000)}\), which becomes \(exit.rate = 1\). Since, \(probation.length = \frac{1}{exit.rate}\), the estimated probation length is 1 year. One way to think about this is that it’s as if all 10,000 probationers from the beginning of the year came off of probation, having completed a full year at some point during the year, and 10,000 new probationers with terms of 1 year came on.

But now imagine the same situation except there are 5,000 exits. Then \(exit.rate = \frac{5,000}{\frac{1}{2}*(10,000+10,000)}\), which becomes \(exit.rate = \frac{1}{2}\). Therefore, \(probation.length = \frac{1}{1/2}\), which is 2 years. One way to think about this is that it’s as if half of the 10,000 probationers from the beginning of the year came off, having completed their second year of probation, the other half has entered their second year, and another 5,000 new probationers with terms of 2 years came on.

Or imagine the same situation except there are 20,000 exits. Then \(exit.rate = \frac{20,000}{\frac{1}{2}*(10,000+10,000)}\), which becomes \(exit.rate = 2\). Therefore, \(probation.length = \frac{1}{2}\), which is 0.5 years. One way to think about this is that it’s as if all 10,000 probationers from the beginning of the year came off of probation, having completed a 1/2 year at some point during the year, another 10,000 new probationers with terms of 1/2 year came on earlier in the year and came off before the end of the year, and another 10,000 new probationers with terms of 1/2 year came on at some point later in the year.

Pew reported data for 2000 and 2018 (see blue dots in Figure 2). Here we fill in some of the gaps by providing data from the BJS Annual Probation Survey for 2012-2017 and 2019-2023.

3 Re-estimating probation term lengths

Although Figure 2 yields a more complete picture of probation term length, there are three known issues with the analysis that motivate a re-analysis.

3.1 Using complete and stable data

3.1.1 Problem: Previous data was skewed because of data entry lag

Since at least 2012 through April of 2024, there appears to be significant data entry lag in Caseload Explorer, the case management system used by probation at the Hawaii State Judiciary. Data entry does not appear to be “stable” until 12-24 months after the end of a calendar year. But prior calculations of probation term length have been based on the BJS data, which in most years was pulled within 6 months after the end of the calendar year. As a result, the number of terminations in a given year has been significantly underestimated, which in term inflates estimates of probation term length (see Section 2).

Expand to learn more about data entry lag in Caseload Explorer

Lag in data entry was discovered when trying to re-analyze probation term lengths and noticing that the number of terminations, or exits, in a given year depends on when the analysis was run. When an analysis for a calendar year is run a few months after the end of that year, as it is typically done for the BJS Annual Probation Survey, there are typically 1000 to 2000 fewer terminations compared to when it is run 24 or more months later.

For instance, when statistics were generated in early 2022 for the 2021 BJS Annual Probation Survey, 3981 terminations were reported based on the Investigation and Supervision Activity reporting tool in Caseload Explorer. When data were reanalyzed in April 2024, there were 4833 terminations, an increase of 852 terminations.

Why did terminations increase by 852? The difference appears to be due in part to data entry lag. Using an in-house script to pull data directly from the underlying database, about 20 supervision cases were sampled that showed up on an extract run in April 2024 but were missing from an earlier extract run in February 2024, and it appears the data for termination date was only added in Caseload Explorer between February and April 2024.

The farther out the analysis is run from the time period under study, the larger the estimated number of terminations tends to get. For example for 2021 terminations, 3981 in early 2022 grows to 4800 in February 2024, which grows to 4833 in April 2024.

The good news is that data appears mostly stable 2-3 years out. In other words, as of today, data from 2021 and anything prior doesn’t seem to change much. But for anything within the past 1-2 years (2022 to 2023), the estimates seem to move a bit because data is still coming in, although it stabilizes the further out one goes.

Furthermore, there is a prediction here. We should expect that if Caseload Explorer reports are rerun for 2022 and 2023 now, in April of 2024, that the number of terminations should not only be higher than when it was run in February 2024 (as was previously shown for the 2021 report), but the increase between February and April for the 2022 Caseload Explorer report should be even greater than 2021, and for the 2023 report, it should be even greater than 2022, as the report is even more recent. For the 2021 report, here’s what we have as our baseline for comparison:

report for 2021 4800 4833 33

The prediction is that the increase for the 2022 report should be greater than 33, and the increase for the 2023 report should be greater than 2022 (whatever it is). If we add the 2022 and 2023 reports, here’s what it looks like:

report Feb-2024 April-2024 Increase
report for 2021 4800 4833 33
report for 2022 5810 5875 65
report for 2023 4130 4280 150

The data is consistent with the predictions. Between 2021 and 2023, there are over 4 times as many “updates” to the termination data for the more recent report. Note, this is between February and April, only two months between checks, which provides some confirmatory evidence that there is a data lag problem, and that it is more severe the more recent the report.

3.1.2 Solution: Using data when it is stable and relatively complete

To address this, the indirect method was used again but with data extracted from Caseload Explorer in April 2024 for each year between 2012 and 2023. For all years except 2022 and 2023, this allowed at least 24 months after the close of the calendar year for probation officers to complete data entry.

3.2 Calculating probation length directly

3.2.1 Problem: The validity of the indirect method depends on assumptions that may have been violated

Without access to individual case-level data, Pew estimated probation term length indirectly using start and end probation population size and number of exits for a given year. This method should deliver valid estimates so long as certain assumptions are met, including that the probation population is stable over time. But the population of probationers in Hawaii has changed more or less sharply, depending on the year.

3.2.2 Solution: Using a direct method to estimate probation term length

For each probationer, a probation term length was calculated by taking the difference between the start date and termination date of their probation term.

\[probationer.term.length = probation.terminate.date - probation.start.date\] This calculation is not based on how long they were ordered to serve by a judge but by how long they actually served.

Each supervision case was assigned to a calendar year based on the year of the termination date. The individual term lengths for cases grouped in a given year were then averaged to get an estimate of probation term length for that year.

\[ probation.term.length_{year} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n({probation.terminate.date_{i,year} - probation.start.date_{i,year}})\] Where i is an individual probationer, n is the total number of probationers in a cohort, and year is a calendar year.

Individual case data was pulled from CX, the database underlying Caseload Explorer.

3.3 Disaggregating felony and non-felony probationers

3.3.1 Problem: The mean of data containing two (mostly) non-overlapping groups is often meaningless

Consider a room filled with 100 government workers and 100 tech industry multi-millionaires. The average salary in the room falls in between the distributions and ends up not representing anyone. When a data distribution is bimodal, reflecting two largely non-overlapping sub-groups, the mean of the entire group is often meaningless.

The probation term lengths for felony and misdemeanor probationers are like salaries in this example. Felony and misdemeanor probationers are in effect two different sub-populations with distinct distributions for term length, and conceptually, mixing them and taking an average can produce a distorted estimate of probation term length. When there are two sub-groups in a dataset, it is often conceptually clearer to disaggregate the data by group and compute separate means for each.

The balance mostly likely depends not on the people convicted of the most or least serious crimes, as they are likely handled similarly across states,2 but on those who have committed crimes of moderate severity. Given this difference between states, should similar probation term lengths be expected in the first instance?

There are reasons for thinking that probation lengths could differ simply because of the probation-incarceration balance. Given a person who has committed a crime, it might be expected that states that have higher probation populations relative to incarceration have longer probation terms because they have more people serving probation who are moderate to high risk. In other words, the same person who might receive a long probation term in one state for comitting a crime of moderate severity might be sentenced to prison in another. But all else equal, the state that sends the person to prison will generally have lower probation term lengths because the offender is not being placed on probation in the first place.

The trouble with comparing probation term lengths across states is that states differ in many ways, not just in the balance between probation and incarceration. The many moving parts between jurisdictions makes comparisons fraught, as observed differences can have many possible explanations, including ones that reveal a difference may be more apparent than real.
